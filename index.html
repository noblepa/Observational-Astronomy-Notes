B. Birth of a star — Nebula
MCQ hot
›
What is a nebula?
A vast interstellar cloud of gas (mainly hydrogen ~75%) and dust. Acts as the raw material and birthplace of all stars.
How it forms a star
Regions of higher density collapse under gravity. Cloud contracts, heats up, spins faster, and forms a rotating disk.
Famous example
Orion Nebula — one of the most active star-forming regions, visible to the naked eye.
Types of nebulae
Emission — glows from ionised gas.
Reflection — reflects nearby starlight.
Dark — blocks background light.
Trigger for collapse: Shockwaves from a nearby supernova can push a nebula to collapse and initiate star formation.
C. Protostar
›
Definition
The earliest observable stage of a star's life. Forms when a dense nebula region collapses under gravity.
Characteristics
Not yet fusing hydrogen. Temperature rising rapidly. Emits infrared radiation. Surrounded by infalling gas and dust.
Duration
Lasts ~100,000 years for Sun-like stars. More massive stars pass through faster.
T Tauri phase
Late protostar stage. Intense stellar winds blow away surrounding gas before nuclear fusion begins.
Key process: Gravitational potential energy converts to heat. The protostar becomes visible only when surrounding dust clears.
D. Main sequence star
MCQ hot
›
How it begins
Core reaches 10 million K, triggering nuclear fusion: hydrogen fuses to helium, releasing enormous energy.
Hydrostatic equilibrium
Outward radiation pressure exactly balances inward gravitational pull — keeps the star stable for billions of years.
The Sun's lifespan
Sun has been on the main sequence for ~4.6 billion years with ~5 billion years remaining.
Mass–lifespan rule
Higher mass = shorter life. 10× Sun mass → ~10 million year life. Low-mass red dwarfs can last trillions of years.
H-R Diagram: Most stars lie on the main sequence diagonal — a plot of luminosity vs temperature. The Sun is a middle-of-the-road G-type main sequence star.
E. Red giant
MCQ hot
›
What triggers it?
Core's hydrogen is exhausted. Fusion stops. Core contracts and heats while outer layers expand and cool.
Appearance
Swells to 100× original diameter, glowing red/orange. Our Sun will expand to engulf Mercury and Venus.
New fusion
Helium fusion (triple-alpha process) begins, producing carbon and oxygen. Massive stars continue fusing heavier elements.
Example
Betelgeuse (Orion) — so large it would extend to Jupiter's orbit if placed where the Sun is.
F. Death stages
MCQ hot
›
Small / Sun-like stars (≤ 8× Sun's mass)
Planetary Nebula: Outer layers gently shed into space, creating a glowing shell. (Not related to planets — historical misnaming.)
White Dwarf: Dense carbon core remains. Size ≈ Earth, mass ≈ Sun. No fusion — shines by residual heat. 1 teaspoon ≈ 1 tonne.
Black Dwarf: White dwarf cools over trillions of years → dark, cold object. None yet exist — universe too young.
Massive stars (> 8× Sun's mass)
Supernova (Type II): Catastrophic collapse + explosion. Can briefly outshine an entire galaxy. Forges heavy elements (gold, lead, uranium).
Neutron Star: ~16 km diameter. Protons + electrons fuse into neutrons. 1 teaspoon ≈ 100 million tonnes.
Pulsar: Rotating neutron star emitting radio wave beams — like a cosmic lighthouse. First detected 1967 by Jocelyn Bell Burnell.
Black Hole: Gravity so extreme even light cannot escape. Boundary = event horizon. Centre = singularity.
Mass determines fate: Low mass → White Dwarf · Medium-high mass → Neutron Star · Extreme mass → Black Hole

A. Classification of galaxies (Hubble Tuning Fork)
MCQ hot
›
Elliptical (E0–E7)
Shape: E0 (sphere) to E7 (flattened ellipse)
Mostly old, red/yellow stars · Very little gas or dust · Almost no new star formation
Example: M87 — giant elliptical, hosts the famous 2019 black hole image
Spiral (Sa–Sc / SBa–SBc)
Flat disk with central bulge + spiral arms and a surrounding halo of old stars
Barred spirals (SB) have a bar of stars through the nucleus. Milky Way is barred.
Examples: Milky Way, Andromeda (M31), Whirlpool (M51)
Irregular (Irr)
No defined shape · Chaotic structure
Rich in gas, dust, and young blue stars · Active star formation
Examples: Large & Small Magellanic Clouds (satellite galaxies of Milky Way)
Hubble's tuning fork: Ellipticals on the handle (E0→E7), splits into normal spirals (Sa→Sc) and barred spirals (SBa→SBc). Irregulars sit off the diagram entirely.

D. Asteroids
›
Definition
Rocky/metallic irregularly shaped bodies, also called minor planets. Too small to be classed as planets.
Main asteroid belt
Located between Mars and Jupiter. Jupiter's gravity prevents them from forming a planet.
Largest
Ceres (~945 km) — classified as a dwarf planet. Others: Vesta, Pallas, Hygiea.
Types
C-type (carbonaceous, most common) · S-type (siliceous/rocky) · M-type (metallic — iron & nickel)
E. Comets
MCQ hot
›
Structure
Nucleus: Dirty snowball of water ice, frozen gases, dust & rock.
Coma: Hazy cloud around nucleus when heated by Sun.
Two tails: Ion tail (plasma) + dust tail.
Tail rule MCQ hot
Tails always point away from the Sun — pushed by solar wind and radiation. NOT behind the comet in its direction of travel.
Origin regions
Kuiper Belt (30–50 AU) → short-period comets (<200 yr orbit)
Oort Cloud (~50,000 AU) → long-period comets (>200 yr orbit)
Famous comets
Halley's Comet — 75–76 yr period, last 1986, next ~2061.
Hale-Bopp — naked-eye visible 18 months (1996–97).
F. Meteoroids, meteors & meteorites
MCQ hot
›
Meteoroid
Small solid particle orbiting the Sun in space. Can be fragments of asteroids or comets.
Meteor MCQ hot
"Shooting star" — streak of light produced when a meteoroid enters Earth's atmosphere and burns due to friction at ~20 km altitude.
Meteorite
Meteoroid that survives the atmosphere and lands on Earth's surface. Types: stony, iron, stony-iron.
Meteor shower
Earth passes through a comet's debris trail. e.g. Perseids (August) from Comet Swift-Tuttle; Leonids (November).

IV. Eclipses
Solar · Lunar · Conditions · Why not monthly
›
Solar eclipse MCQ hot
Alignment: Sun → Moon → Earth
Moon phase: New Moon only
Total: Moon fully covers Sun — narrow path on Earth (umbra shadow)
Partial: Moon covers only part of Sun (penumbra zone)
Annular: Moon farther away → appears smaller → "ring of fire" effect
During totality: corona, prominences, chromosphere become visible
Lunar eclipse MCQ hot
Alignment: Sun → Earth → Moon
Moon phase: Full Moon only
Total: Moon fully in Earth's umbra → turns red ("Blood Moon")
Partial: Only part of Moon enters umbra
Penumbral: Moon in outer shadow only — subtle darkening
Visible from entire night side of Earth — unlike solar eclipse
Why not every month? MCQ hot
Moon's orbit is tilted ~5.1° relative to Earth's orbital plane (ecliptic). At New/Full Moon, Moon usually passes slightly above or below the shadow alignment.
Blood Moon
During total lunar eclipse, Earth's atmosphere scatters blue light and bends red/orange light into Earth's shadow — illuminating the Moon in reddish hues (Rayleigh scattering).
Umbra vs Penumbra
Umbra = total shadow. Penumbra = partial shadow. Antumbra = beyond umbra tip → annular eclipse zone.
Saros cycle
Eclipses repeat on an 18-year, 11-day cycle. Used since ancient times to predict eclipses.
